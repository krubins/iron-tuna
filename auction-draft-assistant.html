What a draft assistant should actually do

An auction is a fast, social, high-pressure event. Names fly, money disappears, and the manager who reacts fastest and most rationally wins. A printed sheet cannot keep up. A real assistant has to do the live math you do not have time to do at the table:

Track every team's budget

See who is flush and who is broke, so you know who can still bid you up and who is forced to bargain hunt.

Recalculate your max after every nomination

Your personal max updates the instant the board moves, so the number you bid to is always current.

Show where the room is overpaying

Spot runs and panic bids in real time and let rivals torch their budgets on names.

Keep your roster legal and balanced

Guidance that respects your open slots and money, so you never get stuck unable to fill a starter.

The three numbers, live

Iron Tuna starts every player with three numbers: market price, true value, and your personal max. The assistant keeps the third number honest as the draft unfolds. Lock up two stud receivers cheaply and your max on the next back climbs, because you can afford to push. Watch the room overpay early and your later maxes rise, because the leftover money chases fewer players. This is auction inflation, calculated live instead of guessed.

Avoid the panic bid

Most auctions are lost on two or three bids that went a little too far. A clear walk-away number for every player is the single most valuable thing a tool can give you, because it converts a stressful judgment call into a simple yes or no. When the bidding crosses your max, you are out, and you move the saved dollars to the next value pocket.
